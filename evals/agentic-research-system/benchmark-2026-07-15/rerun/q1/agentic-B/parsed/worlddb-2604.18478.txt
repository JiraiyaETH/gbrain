Abstract
Persistent memory is the bottleneck separating stateless language-model chatbots from long-running agentic systems. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) backed by flat vector stores fragments facts into chunks, loses cross-session identity, and has no first-class notion of supersession or contradiction. Recent bitemporal knowledge-graph systems (Graphiti, Memento, Hydra DB) address some of these failures with typed edges and valid-time metadata, but the graph itself remains flat—there is no mechanism for recursive composition, no content-addressed invariants on nodes, and edge types carry no behavior beyond a label. on_delete

contradicts

1 Introduction
As language-model deployments shift from stateless chat to long-running agents that span days or months of interaction, the memory layer has become the principal engineering constraint. Context-window scaling addresses none of the underlying problems: latency and cost scale super-linearly with sequence length
[
13
]
, and the “lost-in-the-middle” effect
[
10
]
produces systematic degradation as relevant facts move further from the input boundaries. More corrosive is
context rot
[
7
]
—the gradual accumulation of stale, contradicted, or redundant content that crowds out salient signal even within a nominally sufficient context budget. Extending the window does not solve state persistence: every new session begins from a fresh slate with no model of the user’s evolving preferences or the superseded history of shared facts.
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
[
9
]
was intended to externalize memory, but the canonical implementation inherits three structural weaknesses that have proved difficult to patch:
•
Semantic fragmentation.
Naive chunking destroys cross-segment dependencies. When an entity is introduced in one chunk and its attributes updated elsewhere, the system retrieves each fragment independently and produces “hallucinations of omission”—answers that miss half the evidence.
•
Temporal stagnation.
Flat vector stores are chronology-agnostic. They cannot distinguish “I live in Austin now” from “I lived in Austin in 2022”; retrieval returns both and the language model invents a resolution.
•
Identity drift.
Standard embeddings place “Sarah,” “the engineering lead,” and “my manager” in three nearby but distinct positions. Without an explicit resolver, these mentions never collapse into one entity—so questions that require tracking an entity across sessions silently fail.
Recent bitemporal knowledge-graph systems (Graphiti/Zep

: when a leaf embedding changes, only the path to the root recomposes.
Content vs. effective embedding split.
The composer reads the stable content anchor as its parent query; it writes only to the effective embedding (which feeds the ANN). Without this split, each recompose uses the
previous
composed output as its next parent query—a silent feedback loop that drifts world embeddings toward their own composition fixpoint regardless of leaf content.
6 Background Consolidator
Over the lifetime of a long-running store, the graph grows in three predictable ways: (i) leaves accumulate, (ii) latent facts go unrecorded because no single turn stated them, (iii) contradictions emerge between distant regions that never interacted at write time. The consolidator is the engine’s “sleep cycle”—a background process that addresses all three:
•
Summary generation.
Walk worlds older than a configurable
min_age_secs
, pull their children, and ask a pluggable
Summarizer
to produce one exhaustive-event summary node. Write
World
→
\to
Summary
→
\to
details
so queries with
prefer_summaries()
return the summary at the default depth and only descend on demand.
•
Inferred transitive edges.
Compute one-pass closure on
causes
and
subtype_of
. New edges are flagged
metadata.inferred=true
and routed through the standard handler pipeline.
•
Contradiction sweep.
Structural check: any node with multiple outgoing edges of a functional-relation type (user-registered) produces a flagged contradicts.
At a
1000
1000
-fact,
100
100
-topic scale the summary-preferred query runs
6.5
×
6.5\times
faster
than the full-detail traversal (
30
30
ms vs.
194
194
ms, median of
9
9
runs), and the detail remains reachable on demand—the “default-depth view is the summary” semantics are preserved without destroying the underlying leaves.

9 Discussion and Limitations
Provenance over silence.
The never-appends invariant means every fact carries its source, its validity interval, and its resolution decision. This is more expensive than a flat store at ingest time—the resolver tiers cost
5
5
-
15
15
ms per candidate at
10
10
k-node scale—but eliminates the “silent corruption” failure mode where a flat store unknowingly contains two conflicting facts and returns one at random.
Composed embeddings need training.
The v2 attention aggregator is parameter-free. A natural next step is a learned aggregator trained via contrastive loss on query
→
\to
correct-world pairs, in the style of HAKG
[
5
]
; we consider this open work.
DMR benchmark.
We evaluate on LongMemEval-s but defer the DMR benchmark
[
4
]
. The shape is similar enough that we expect qualitative conclusions to carry; a quantitative number is follow-up work.
Bio-mimetic decay.
The Bio-Mimetic Decay Engine proposed in
[
12
]
—retention scores with age decay and retrieval-frequency reinforcement—is architecturally compatible with our consolidator’s interface but not yet implemented. We believe it is a natural next step.

WorldDB
is a memory engine for agents built on three commitments that flat RAG and even recent bitemporal KG systems decline to make: recursive worlds, content-addressed immutability, and edges as write-time programs. Together they produce a substrate where the graph topology

Each (question, reference, generated) triple is scored independently by a single Sonnet 4.6 call. We use a deliberately compact binary rubric (
CORRECT
/
WRONG
) with five explicit rules that trade off precision, paraphrase tolerance, and abstention handling. Unlike multi-dimensional scorers that ask the judge to emit JSON with

Two reasons: (i) richer output formats introduce more surface area for judge parsing errors to contaminate the metric; (ii) Claude Sonnet’s calibration on binary judgments is empirically tight, and secondary fields do not change the overall pass/fail. The

Verdict parsing.
The judge’s response is

-ed and matched by prefix: if it
startswith("CORRECT")

Calibration tests.
Three cases of the rubric are load-bearing and worth flagging:
1.
Reference admits ignorance.
When the LongMemEval reference itself says “The information provided is not enough…”, a generated
"I don’t know"
must score CORRECT. Early versions of our judge prompt (v8 in the ablation trace) marked this WRONG, dragging knowledge-update accuracy by
∼
3
\sim 3
pp. Rule three is the explicit fix.
2.
Paraphrase tolerance.
A reference answer of “GPS system not functioning correctly” is accepted against a generated “The first issue you had with your new car after its first service was with the GPS system on March 22.”
3.
Numeric strictness.
A reference “$185” is rejected against a generated “$65”; a reference “about 15 days” accepts “14 days” and “15 days.” Rule four makes the difference between an approximation judged present in the reference and an arbitrary rounding by the model.
Why not an ensemble judge?
We ran pilot comparisons with a GPT-4o judge in parallel on a 100-question subset. The two agreed on
94
94
% of verdicts; the
6
6
% disagreements were almost entirely preference questions where subjective quality ratings diverge by judge (cf. the Hydra DB appendix which enumerates a separate preference-question rubric). We report Sonnet 4.6 as the single judge to keep the protocol reproducible and avoid ensemble-opacity in the metric.
Appendix B
Why No LLM in the Engine
The engine never calls a language model on the read path. This reflects two observations:
1.
LLM latency and cost are incompatible with query-path SLOs. A
200
200
ms answerer call multiplies by every hop of a multi-step query. At a
500
500
ms P95 target there is no budget for it.
2.
LLM non-determinism corrupts the ontology. The reconciler’s guarantees rely on handler code being a function of input state; an LLM call breaks that, and the failure mode is silent.
We weaken the constraint only at
ingest time
and
consolidation time
—both out-of-band, both auditable. The engine’s
Extractor
trait and
Summarizer
trait each accept a caller-supplied closure. Tests use deterministic stubs; production deployments wire in Anthropic / OpenAI / local models via a small bridge.
