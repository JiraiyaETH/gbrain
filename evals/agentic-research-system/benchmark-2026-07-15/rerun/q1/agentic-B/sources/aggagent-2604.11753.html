Agentic Aggregation for Parallel Scaling of Long-Horizon Agentic Tasks

Yoonsang Lee   Howard Yen   Xi Ye   Danqi Chen Affiliation: Princeton Language    Intelligence    Princeton University Affiliation: {yoonsang, hyen, danqic}@cs.princeton.edu   xi.ye@princeton.edu
Abstract

We study parallel test-time scaling for long-horizon agentic tasks such as agentic search and deep research, where multiple rollouts are generated in parallel and aggregated into a final response. While such scaling has proven effective for chain-of-thought reasoning, agentic tasks pose unique challenges: trajectories are long, multi-turn, and tool-augmented, and outputs are often open-ended. Aggregating only final answers discards rich information from trajectories, while concatenating all trajectories exceeds the model’s context window. To address this, we propose AggAgent, an aggregation agent that treats parallel trajectories as an environment. We equip it with lightweight tools to inspect candidate solutions and search across trajectories, enabling it to navigate and synthesize information on demand. Across six benchmarks and three model families (GLM-4.7, Qwen3.5, MiniMax-M2.5), AggAgent outperforms all existing aggregation methods—by up to 5.3% absolute on average and 10.3% on two deep research tasks—while adding minimal overhead, as the aggregation cost remains bounded by a single agentic rollout. Our findings establish agentic aggregation as an effective and cost-efficient approach to parallel test-time scaling.111Our code is available at https://github.com/princeton-pli/AggAgent.

1 Introduction

Scaling test-time compute has emerged as a promising avenue for enhancing the performance of large language models (LLMs) (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2024; Welleck et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2025; Muennighoff et al., 2025). This success has largely been demonstrated in standard chain-of-thought (CoT) tasks such as mathematical reasoning and coding (Qi et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025). However, long-horizon agentic tasks

(e.g., deep research, software engineering, web navigation) present a fundamentally different challenge: trajectories are multi-turn, spanning hundreds of steps with interleaved tool calls and observations (Yao et al., 2022b).

In this work, we study parallel scaling for long-horizon agentic tasks, an approach proven effective for CoT reasoning tasks yet largely unexplored for agentic search and deep research. Parallel scaling enables simultaneous generation of multiple independent trajectories, offering a natural computational advantage (Zhao et al., 2025). Furthermore, parallel scaling offers substantial improvement over single-agent performance (Wei et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026): for instance, GLM-4.7-Flash (Zeng et al., 2025) improves from 27% to 59% on BrowseComp (Wei et al., 2025) and 25% to 51% on HLE (Phan et al., 2025) when scaling from Pass@1 to Pass@8, confirming that correct solutions frequently exist within the parallel rollouts.

The central question then becomes how to effectively aggregate these trajectories. While voting and solution aggregation methods have proven effective for mathematical reasoning and coding (Wang et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2026; Zhao et al., 2025), long-horizon agentic tasks pose distinct challenges: evidence is sparse and distributed across multi-turn trajectories, demanding reasoning far beyond shallow heuristics and final solutions (Li et al., 2025a). Furthermore, individual trajectories may each capture only partial progress toward a complex task, requiring cross-trajectory synthesis to assemble a complete solution (Chang et al., 2026). However, existing approaches fall short (Table 1): aggregating only final solutions discards the rich information in trajectories, summarizing trajectories is expensive and lossy, and concatenating all trajectories into a single context is infeasible, as each trajectory spans hundreds of thousands of tokens.

To address these challenges, we propose AggAgent, which frames aggregation itself as an agentic task, treating the set of trajectories as an environment to interact with (Figure 2). AggAgent interacts with trajectories on demand through three lightweight tools: get_solution (retrieve final solutions of one or all trajectories), search_trajectory (keyword search within a trajectory), and get_segment (read a specific range of steps). Since these tools operate entirely over an array of completed trajectories—rather than invoking external services like web search or code execution—they incur none of the API cost or latency of such external tools. This agentic aggregation allows for cross-trajectory reasoning and synthesis without the cost of loading all trajectories into context, preserving full fidelity while keeping the aggregation cost bounded by a single agentic rollout.

We evaluate AggAgent on six agentic search and deep research benchmarks across three model families (GLM-4.7, Qwen3.5, MiniMax-M2.5). AggAgent consistently outperforms all baselines (Figure 1), improving performance by up to 5.3 points on average and 10.3 points on deep research tasks. Furthermore, AggAgent is Pareto-optimal in cost and performance (Figure 3), adding only a 5.7% overhead over the cost of running 8 parallel agents—compared to 41% for Summary Aggregation—by selectively reading portions of trajectories rather than loading them entirely into context. Notably, AggAgent even surpasses Pass@8 (Section 6.1), demonstrating that effective aggregation can synthesize correct solutions beyond what any single rollout achieves. To summarize, our results show that agentic aggregation is a scalable and effective paradigm for test-time scaling in long-horizon tasks. We demonstrate that AggAgent is effective with off-the-shelf LLMs, and our framework further opens a promising direction for training aggregator agents.

2 Problem Formulation

We study long-horizon tasks such as agentic search and deep research. Given a problem qq, the goal is to produce an output yy, which may be either a short answer, a set of answers, or a long-form report. An agent AA interacts with an external environment to solve qq by generating a trajectory T=(q,r1,a1,o1,…,rm,am,om,y)T=(q,r_{1},a_{1},o_{1},\dots,r_{m},a_{m},o_{m},y), where each step consists of internal thinking rjr_{j}, tool call aja_{j}, and resulting observation ojo_{j}. In parallel scaling, we run the same agent independently for KK times, yielding 𝒯={T1,T2,…,TK}\mathcal{T}=\{T_{1},T_{2},\dots,T_{K}\}. This setting is natural in production deployments of frontier systems (Anthropic, 2025; Cursor, 2025; Kimi Team et al., 2026), as the latency is determined by the slowest of the KK rollouts.

The core problem centers around how to aggregate these KK trajectories and synthesize a better solution yy. We formalize aggregation as a function f:(q,𝒯)↦y^f:(q,\mathcal{T})\mapsto\hat{y}, where y^\hat{y} is the aggregated final solution. For agentic search, y^\hat{y} is evaluated against the ground-truth answer y∗y^{*}, while for deep research, it is evaluated against problem-specific rubrics.

Existing methods

We adopt the following aggregation methods from prior works:

  • •

    Majority Voting (MV) (Wang et al., 2023): selects the most frequent solution. However, voting methods do not work for multi-answer or long-form generation tasks.

  • •

    Best-of-N (BoN) (Cobbe et al., 2021; Uesato et al., 2022): selects the solution with the highest self-reported confidence. While reward model scores are commonly used as the weight for CoT reasoning tasks, recent works on agentic tasks have adopted the model’s self-reported confidence as the weight (Wei et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2026).

  • •

    Weighted Majority Voting (WMV) (Li et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024): selects the solution with the highest total weight, where each solution is weighted by self-reported confidence.

  • •

    Fewest Tool Calls (FewTool) (Liu et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025): selects the solution from the trajectory that required the fewest tool calls.

  • •

    Solution Aggregation (SolAgg) (Qi et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025; Qiao et al., 2025): concatenates all KK solutions and prompts an LLM to synthesize a final solution.

  • •

    Summary Aggregation (SummAgg) (Li et al., 2025a; Hu et al., 2026): compresses each trajectory into a summary report, concatenates all reports, and prompts an LLM to generate a final solution.

We classify these baselines into two groups. Heuristic methods (MV, WMV, BoN, FewTool) rely on shallow signals such as answer frequency, confidence scores, or trajectory length, without inspecting trajectory content. LLM-based methods (SolAgg, SummAgg) leverage LLM reasoning for solution synthesis but remain limited: Solution Aggregation aggregates only final solutions, discarding all intermediate evidence, while Summary Aggregation compresses each trajectory into a summary report before aggregation, incurring irreversible information loss and requiring KK additional LLM calls. Table 1 summarizes these limitations.

3 Our Approach: AggAgent

To address the aforementioned limitations, we propose AggAgent, where the aggregator interacts with 𝒯\mathcal{T} directly in an agentic manner. The initial user message provides the problem qq and trajectory metadata, which consists of the number of steps, total token count, and tool usage statistics for each trajectory TiT_{i}. The trajectories themselves are not pre-loaded into the context but reside in the environment and are retrieved on-demand, keeping the aggregation cost bounded by a single context window independent of KK. This design enables cross-trajectory reasoning at full fidelity and low cost (Table 1).

Tool design

We provide AggAgent with four tools as follows222We provide the full tool descriptions in Figure 18 in the appendix.:

  • •

    get_solution(traj_id): Retrieves the final solution from each trajectory’s last step. Unless traj_id is specified, returns all KK solutions by default.

  • •

    search_trajectory(traj_id, query, role, k): Searches for keywords within a single trajectory. Returns the top matching kk steps ranked by ROUGE-L score (Lin, 2004).

  • •

    get_segment(traj_id, start_step, end_step): Reads the full content of a contiguous range of steps from a single trajectory. Returns raw thinking and tool observations for the specified window.

  • •

    finish(): Submits the final solution along with a reason for the aggregation.

Workflow

The tool design reflects a natural coarse-to-fine investigative workflow. Rather than reading every trajectory in full, the agent first surveys the metadata and solutions loaded by get_solution to identify consensus and disagreements across the rollouts, and to pinpoint trajectories worth closer inspection. It then selectively dives into individual trajectories via search_trajectory and get_segment to verify key claims against thinking blocks and tool observations. After sufficient cross-trajectory validation, it calls finish to submit the final synthesized solution. We provide the full prompts in Appendix B.

Cost analysis

Aggregation cost is measured in additional LLM calls beyond the KK rollouts. Heuristic methods require zero additional calls, while Solution Aggregation requires a single LLM call to synthesize the KK solution candidates. Summary Aggregation is the most expensive, as it requires KK separate LLM calls before aggregation, each of which can be as long as the maximum context window. In contrast, AggAgent’s total context is bounded by a single context window of the model, keeping aggregation cost independent of KK and comparable to Solution Aggregation. Moreover, as AggAgent’s tools operate entirely over the in-memory trajectory array, they incur none of the latency or cost of external tools like web search and code execution.

4 Experimental Setup

Tasks

We evaluate on six long-horizon agentic tasks broadly in two categories: 1) Agentic search: BrowseComp (Wei et al., 2025) consists of challenging factual questions demanding exhaustive multi-step web browsing; BrowseComp-Plus (Chen et al., 2025) enables controlled evaluation on BrowseComp questions by replacing web search with a local knowledge base; HLE (Phan et al., 2025) covers expert-level questions across diverse academic disciplines, emphasizing rigorous reasoning; DeepSearchQA (Gupta et al., 2026) targets multi-answer queries where completeness across all valid answers is required. 2) Deep research: Healthbench-Hard (Arora et al., 2025) requires generating comprehensive long-form responses to challenging medical queries; ResearchRubrics (Sharma et al., 2025) poses open-ended research tasks evaluated against detailed, multi-criterion rubrics.

Due to high costs of long-horizon rollouts, we randomly sample subsets for evaluation333We report standard deviation in Appendix C.1.. For HLE, we use 155 search-focused questions (Li et al., 2025a). For ResearchRubrics, we use the full 101 problems. For Healthbench-Hard, we randomly sample 100 instances. For all other tasks, we randomly sample 150 questions following Sun et al. (2025); Zhang et al. (2025a).

Models

We employ three model families of varying sizes for base trajectory rollouts: GLM-4.7-Flash (30B) (Zeng et al., 2025), Qwen3.5-122B-A10B (122B) (Qwen Team, 2026), and MiniMax-M2.5 (229B) (MiniMax, 2026). We adopt Tongyi DeepResearch (Tongyi DeepResearch Team et al., 2025) as the agent scaffold, use native function calling, allow at most 128K context length with 100 tool calls, and sample 8 independent trajectories. For LLM-based aggregation, we use the same model as the rollout agent.

Rollout agent tools

For BrowseComp-Plus, we follow the official implementation and provide search and get_document tools using Qwen3-Embedding-8B (Zhang et al., 2025b) as the retriever. For all other tasks, we provide search and visit tools following prior work (Yen et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025b; Wu et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2026), where search uses Serper API444https://serper.dev for Google search and visit uses crawl4ai555https://github.com/unclecode/crawl4ai to scrape a specific web page.

Cost and latency

We report total cost and latency per query. Cost includes rollout, tool calls, and aggregation, while latency includes rollout and aggregation.

Evaluation

We employ LLM-as-a-judge, following the official evaluation setting. Specifically, we use Qwen3.5-397B-A17B for ResearchRubrics and GPT-4.1 (Achiam et al., 2023) for all other datasets. We perform bootstrapped sampling for calculating Metric@K∈{1,2,4,8}K\in\{1,2,4,8\}. For BrowseComp, BrowseComp-Plus, and HLE, the predicted answer y^\hat{y} is evaluated against the gold answer y∗y^{*}. For DeepSearchQA, the predicted answer set is considered correct if it exactly matches the gold answer set. For Healthbench-Hard and ResearchRubrics, the long-form response is evaluated across multiple problem-specific rubrics, and the scores are averaged. The rubrics contain negative scores, hence the total score could also be negative.

We provide further experimental details in Appendix A.

5 Results

5.1 Main Results

We present the main results on K=8K=8 in Table 2, and present the full results (K∈{1,2,4,8}K\in\{1,2,4,8\}) in Appendix C.1. AggAgent consistently outperforms all baselines across all three models, improving over Pass@1 by 13.3–17.9 points and over the strongest baseline (SolAgg) by 2.4–5.3 points in average score.

Among the heuristic baselines, Majority Voting performs the worst, while confidence-based methods (WMV, BoN) yield substantial improvements, consistent with prior observations (Wei et al., 2025). However, the gains are less pronounced on DeepSearchQA, Healthbench-Hard, and ResearchRubrics, where the model is poorly calibrated (see Appendix C.4 for details). Notably, voting methods are not applicable to multi-answer or long-form tasks, so their performance gains are limited to single-answer tasks only.666We use Pass@1 instead to calculate the average score. Fewest Tool Calls has been popular on BrowseComp due to its simplicity (Liu et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025), but our results show that it also does not generalize well beyond such tasks.

Meanwhile, LLM-based methods consistently outperform heuristic baselines. Summary Aggregation outperforms all other baselines on agentic search tasks, as access to full trajectory evidence aids answer verification. However, it falls significantly behind Solution Aggregation on deep research tasks, as trajectory compression harms the detail and coherence of long-form responses. While no single baseline is universally optimal, AggAgent achieves strong performance by enabling fine-grained reasoning over trajectories without sacrificing detail or coherence.

5.2 Cost and Latency vs. Performance

We compare cost and latency versus performance for different numbers of samples K∈{2,4,8}K\in\{2,4,8\} in Figure 3 (full breakdown in Appendix C.2). As KK increases, all methods become more expensive and slower due to additional rollouts. AggAgent and Solution Aggregation introduce only minimal overhead beyond heuristic baselines—at K=8K{=}8, their overhead over the rollout cost is 5.7% and 3.7%, respectively—yet substantially outperform them. Interestingly, their overhead does not increase proportionally to KK, suggesting that having more samples can sometimes make aggregation easier by providing stronger supporting evidence, partially offsetting the extra computation spent on harder instances. In contrast, Summary Aggregation incurs 41% overhead at K=8K{=}8 because it must compress each trajectory before aggregation. Overall, AggAgent achieves Pareto-optimal performance and efficiency across benchmarks, showing that agentic aggregation scales efficiently with test-time compute without incurring substantial additional cost.

6 Analysis and Discussion

6.1 Stronger Models for Aggregation

Could aggregation benefit from employing a stronger model? We investigate this by replacing the aggregator with MiniMax-M2.5 while keeping GLM-4.7-Flash as the base rollout agent. As shown in Figure 4 (full results in Table 11 in the appendix), using a stronger aggregator generally improves performance, with MiniMax-based AggAgent achieving the highest average score and even surpassing Pass@8 on BrowseComp-Plus. This points to asymmetric model allocation—a stronger model for aggregation, weaker models for parallel rollouts—as a practical strategy for designing multi-agent systems, consistent with prior work employing stronger models as orchestrators over multiple weaker subagents (Qiao et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a; Akay et al., 2026). In our setting, the aggregator plays the orchestrating role, reasoning across parallel trajectories to produce a final solution.

6.2 Output Design Ablation: Synthesis vs. Selection

We ablate the output design of AggAgent by comparing it against a selection variant, which selects the single best trajectory and adopts its solution directly instead of synthesizing a new solution. As shown in Figure 5, AggAgent performs better overall, though the selection variant remains competitive in some settings. Notably, the selection variant performs poorly on deep research benchmarks, where AggAgent outperforms it by a large margin. We attribute this to a mismatch between selection and the nature of open-ended research tasks: quality is distributed across trajectories so no single trajectory dominates, yet selection forces an all-or-nothing commitment. Synthesis mitigates this by making local, compositional judgments about which parts of each trajectory to incorporate, rather than committing to a global winner. This distinction is less pronounced for agentic search, where trajectories tend to be clearly correct or clearly wrong, making selection more straightforward. These results suggest that synthesis is often the preferable output design, consistent with our findings in Section 6.1 and 6.4 that AggAgent can compose a correct solution from individually incorrect trajectories. We leave a more thorough ablation of output design to future work.

6.3 Tool Usage Patterns

In this section, we analyze the tool usage behaviour of AggAgent. As depicted in Figure 6, search_trajectory accounts for the majority of tool calls, while get_solution and finish are each invoked approximately once per query, consistent with the intended workflow: solutions are surveyed once at the start and the agent terminates after the final synthesis. Meanwhile, get_segment is used relatively less than search_trajectory, suggesting that AggAgent reserves full-content reads for cases where keyword-level search alone is insufficient, diving deep only when actually needed. We also observe that stronger models tend to require fewer tool calls overall, suggesting more capable models reach a final solution more efficiently. Deep research tasks similarly exhibit fewer tool calls, potentially due to less need for resolving precise factual conflicts across trajectories.

6.4 Qualitative Analysis

We qualitatively analyze cases where AggAgent succeeds in producing correct answers. AggAgent demonstrates four key behaviours (Figure 7): (1) Minority answer identification: identifying the correct answer from multiple trajectories, even when it is held by only a minority, (2) Disagreement resolution: resolving inconsistencies and conflicts across trajectories to arrive at a coherent answer, (3) Cross-trajectory synthesis: constructing a correct solution by reasoning over trajectories that are all incorrect, and (4) Heuristic interpretation: leveraging heuristic signals such as majority vote and confidence scores to guide aggregation, while not relying on them entirely. Together, these behaviours highlight AggAgent’s capacity for nuanced, evidence-driven reasoning across parallel trajectories beyond simple voting or selection.

7 Related Work

Long-horizon agents

LLM agents have emerged as a powerful paradigm, extending language models beyond pure parametric generation to interactive problem solving in external environments. ReAct (Yao et al., 2022b) has been the most prominent workflow, interleaving reasoning, action selection, and environment observations. This has enabled a wide range of applications, including agentic RAG (Jin et al., 2025b; Li et al., 2025c; Jin et al., 2025a), software engineering (Jimenez et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025), web navigation (Yao et al., 2022a; Chae et al., 2025; Gandhi & Neubig, 2026), and deep research systems (OpenAI, 2025; Anthropic, 2025; Roucher et al., 2025). Recent work has sought to improve agentic capabilities through advances in RL algorithms (Shao et al., 2024; 2025; Ritter et al., 2026), agentic mid-training (Su et al., 2025), and environment scaling (Fang et al., 2025). Our work is complementary to these efforts: we treat these agents as rollout models and study how to aggregate parallel trajectories into a single high-quality solution.

Test-time scaling

Early work on test-time scaling for CoT tasks (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022) such as math reasoning and coding showed clear gains from search-based strategies (Yao et al., 2023), extended thinking budgets (Muennighoff et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025; Snell et al., 2025), majority voting (Wang et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2024), and learning-based aggregation (Qi et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025).

However, test-time scaling for long-horizon agents is more challenging, as trajectories are lengthy, heterogeneous, and difficult to compare using simple surface-level signals (e.g., majority or confidence). Parallel scaling with LLM-based aggregation mitigates this problem by generating multiple independent trajectories and reasoning over them to produce a final solution. WebResearcher (Qiao et al., 2025) concatenates final answers and prompts an LLM to synthesize a solution, while ParallelMuse (Li et al., 2025a) compresses each trajectory into a summary report before aggregation, incurring high cost and information loss. Concurrent with our work, KARL (Chang et al., 2026) feeds final answers back to the rollout agent with the same tools enabled, but it disregards full trajectories, and reusing rollout tools can be costly in practice. In contrast, AggAgent enables the aggregator to interact with trajectories in an agentic manner, supporting cross-trajectory reasoning without lossy compression or excessive cost. While AggAgent is training-free and effective with off-the-shelf LLMs, we leave fine-tuning the aggregator as a promising direction for future work.

Orthogonal to our focus on parallel scaling, several works have studied sequential scaling to extend the effective horizon of a single agent through context management (Wu et al., 2025; Yen et al., 2025; Tang et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2026), context folding (Sun et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2026), or iterative refinement (Zhu et al., 2026; Xiao et al., 2026).

8 Conclusion

We introduce AggAgent, an aggregation agent for parallel scaling of long-horizon agentic tasks. By treating parallel trajectories as an interactive environment and navigating them via lightweight in-memory tools, AggAgent enables cross-trajectory reasoning at full fidelity, while avoiding both the information loss of compression and the prohibitive cost of loading all trajectories into context. Across six benchmarks and three models, AggAgent consistently outperforms all baselines, achieving Pareto-optimal performance and efficiency. Our findings establish agentic aggregation as a principled and cost-efficient paradigm for parallel test-time scaling.

Appendix A Experimental Details

A.1 Implementation

We serve all models via vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) and use the Tongyi DeepResearch scaffold (Tongyi DeepResearch Team et al., 2025) for agentic workflow. We enable interleaved reasoning and native function calling, with temperature 1.0, top-pp 0.95, a 128K context window, 10K max output tokens, and at most 100 tool calls per rollout. Upon exhausting the context limit or tool call budget, the model is given one final opportunity to submit an answer without tool access. All experiments are conducted on 4×\timesH100 GPUs.

A.2 Rollout Agent Tools

BrowseComp-Plus The local corpus contains 100,195 documents. search uses Qwen3-Embedding-8B to retrieve the top-5 documents by score, with each snippet truncated to 512 tokens. get_document returns the full text of a document by docid, truncated to 4,096 tokens following Su et al. (2025).

  • •

    search(query): Perform a search on a knowledge source. Returns top-5 hits with docid, score, and snippet. The snippet contains the document’s contents (may be truncated based on token limits).

  • •

    get_document(docid): Retrieve a full document by its docid.

All other datasets We adopt the tool implementation from SLIM (Yen et al., 2025). search queries Google Search via the Serper API (https://serper.dev), returning the top-10 results with snippets of at most 150 characters. visit scrapes a URL via crawl4ai (https://github.com/unclecode/crawl4ai) and extracts the snippet most relevant to a goal, ranked by ROUGE-L score (Lin, 2004).

  • •

    search(query): Performs a web search: supply a string ‘query’; the tool retrieves the top 10 results for the query.

  • •

    visit(url, goal): Visit a webpage and return the relevant content based on the goal.

A.3 Cost Calculation

Let gi​ng_{in} and go​u​tg_{out} denote the per-token input and output costs of a language model, and let gsg_{s} and gvg_{v} denote the per-call costs of search and visit, respectively. The total cost per question is c=cr+ct+cac=c_{r}+c_{t}+c_{a}.

Rollout cost (crc_{r}): Following Yen et al. (2025), cached input tokens are excluded from the token count, as caching is standard in long-trajectory settings with shared context. The total rollout cost is cr=∑i(gi​n⋅Δ​ti​n(i)+go​u​t⋅to​u​t(i))c_{r}=\sum_{i}\left(g_{in}\cdot\Delta t_{in}^{(i)}+g_{out}\cdot t_{out}^{(i)}\right), where Δ​ti​n(i)=ti​n(i)−ti​n(i−1)\Delta t_{in}^{(i)}=t_{in}^{(i)}-t_{in}^{(i-1)}.

Tool call cost (ctc_{t}): For BrowseComp-Plus, which uses a local corpus, ct=0c_{t}=0. For all other tasks, ct=gs⋅ns+gv⋅nvc_{t}=g_{s}\cdot n_{s}+g_{v}\cdot n_{v}, where nsn_{s} and nvn_{v} are the total numbers of search and visit calls.

Aggregation cost (cac_{a}): Only LLM-based aggregation methods incur additional aggregation cost. For Solution Aggregation, cac_{a} is a single LLM call: gi​n⋅ti​n+go​u​t⋅to​u​tg_{in}\cdot t_{in}+g_{out}\cdot t_{out}. For Summary Aggregation, this is summed over K+1K+1 calls. For AggAgent, cac_{a} is computed identically to crc_{r}, as it forms an agentic trajectory.

Cost is averaged over all instances for each benchmark. Pricing rates are given in Table 3. Prices are sourced from the respective provider websites: GLM-4.7-Flash (https://docs.z.ai/guides/overview/pricing), Qwen3.5-122B (https://openrouter.ai/qwen/qwen3.5-122b-a10b), MiniMax-M2.5 (https://platform.minimax.io/docs/guides/pricing-paygo), and tool calls (https://www.firecrawl.dev/pricing).

A.4 Latency Measurement

We measure latency by processing one problem at a time, using 2×\timesH100 for GLM-4.7-Flash and 4×\timesH100 for Qwen3.5-122B and MiniMax-M2.5.

Rollout latency (ℓr\ell_{r}): Wall-clock time from the first step of the trajectory until termination.

Aggregation latency (ℓa\ell_{a}): For Solution Aggregation, the time of the single LLM call. For Summary Aggregation, we spawn KK summarization calls in parallel, wait for all to complete, then issue the final aggregation call; ℓa\ell_{a} covers the full span. For AggAgent, ℓa\ell_{a} is measured the same way as ℓr\ell_{r}.

For latency, we sample 30 instances and report the median for efficiency, as latency must be measured sequentially. All other experiments run with batched generation.

C.4 Confidence Calibration

While prior work has demonstrated the effectiveness of confidence-based aggregation strategies such as Weighted Majority Voting and Best-of-N (Wei et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a), we find that these methods do not consistently generalize across benchmarks. As illustrated in Figure 11, all three models show well-calibrated confidence on BrowseComp-Plus and BrowseComp (large C−WC-W gap), but are less calibrated on HLE and DeepSearchQA. Moreover, on Healthbench-Hard and ResearchRubrics, confidence is essentially uncorrelated with quality (r≈0r\approx 0). We hypothesize that this miscalibration explains why confidence-based methods plateau or even degrade as KK increases on those tasks. In contrast, LLM-based aggregation, and especially AggAgent, scales reliably with KK across all benchmarks.
